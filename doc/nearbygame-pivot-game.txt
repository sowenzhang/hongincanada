# How I Pivoted NearbyGame (and Used AI to Go From 0 → 1)

A few weeks ago, NearbyGame was something completely different.

It wasn’t a game.

It was a place to find badminton and table tennis venues nearby — something like a mix between Yelp and Meetup. The idea sounded reasonable: help people discover places and connect to play.

But something felt off.

I didn’t have the energy to manually collect venue data. There is also no easy way to scrap data from Internet. Free sources such as openstreetmap have a lot of missing and false information. I had to build an ingestion pipeline with a lot of manual intervention. 
But more importantly — it didn’t feel exciting to build.

(insert image: previous_nearbygame_home.jpg and previous_nearbygame_venuepage.jpg) 

So I paused.

And instead of forcing execution, I started using AI to **think**.

---

## Step 1: Realizing the problem wasn’t execution

At first, I thought:

> “Maybe I just need to push harder — collect more data, improve SEO, add more features.”

But after a few conversations (with AI as a sounding board), it became clear:

This wasn’t an execution problem.
It was a **problem selection problem**.

The original idea had hidden constraints:

* required manual data (venues)
* required two-sided marketplace (players + places)
* low engagement frequency (people don’t play every day)
* depended on network effects too early

That’s a tough place to start.

AI didn’t give me the answer — but it helped me **see the structure of the problem clearly**.

---

## Step 2: Breaking the idea down

Instead of asking:

> “How do I fix NearbyGame?”

I started asking:

> “What part of this idea actually matters?”

Through iterative conversations, I decomposed it into:

* “nearby” → proximity
* “game” → interaction
* “people” → connection

Then I questioned each:

Do I really need:

* real-world venues?
* scheduling?
* coordination?

The answer was no.

Those were the hard parts.

---

## Step 3: Reframing the idea

This was the turning point.

Instead of:

> “Help people organize games in real life”

I shifted to:

> “What if people are already *in a shared experience*, and connection happens naturally?”

That’s a completely different direction.

---

## Step 4: From coordination → encounter

The old idea required effort:

* find a place
* find a time
* find people

The new idea removes all of that.

You just:

* open the app
* start walking
* occasionally encounter someone

And only then:

* decide if you want to interact

No pressure. No planning.

---

## Step 5: The new concept

NearbyGame is now:

> A quiet walking experience where you might meet someone.

You start in a foggy world.

* You walk forward
* You explore abstract “cities”
* You accumulate distance over time

There’s no map. No specific destination.

Just progress.

And sometimes…

You see another player.

You don’t know them.
You don’t have to talk.

You can:

* ignore
* acknowledge
* or send a simple signal: “Game?”

If both agree, you jump into a small shared game.

Then you continue your walk.

---

## Step 6: Designing for single-player first

This was one of the biggest lessons.

The original idea only worked if multiple users showed up at the same time.

The new version works with one user.

That changes everything.

Now:

* no dependency on network effects
* no cold start problem
* no need for growth before value

AI helped reinforce this principle again and again:

> “Make it meaningful for one user first.”

I actually love this idea, and to be honestly, I was having fun when testing the app. 

---

## Step 7: Using AI to go from idea → structure → system

AI wasn’t just helping me brainstorm.

It helped me:

### 1. Clarify constraints

* what to avoid (marketplaces, scheduling, coordination)
* what to focus on (solo loop, lightweight interaction)

### 2. Define architecture

* What platform to start with
* What technology used for frontend, backend, and cross-platform
* What telemetry is crucial to collect without burning server load
* What security I should pay attention to

### 3. Break work into systems

* world rendering
* session system
* progression system
* map system

### 4. Create a build framework

I even generated:

* CLAUDE.md (project philosophy)
* agents (modular roles)
* skills (reusable capabilities)
* story.md (product narrative)

This turned a vague idea into something I could actually build.

---

## Step 8: Building the first version

Instead of jumping into a full 3D engine, I chose:

* lightweight rendering (web-based and 25D)
* simple foggy road
* abstract city nodes

Why?

Because the goal is not graphics.

The goal is to answer one question:

> “Can I create something simple for people to come back?”

---

## Step 9: Adding meaning — progression

One small feature made the experience feel real:

> Landmark discovery
> Area exploration

As you walk, your distance converts into:

- “You have explored 6.5% of the Washington state”

or 

- "You have seen Space Needle" 

It’s abstract, but motivating.

It gives:

* direction without pressure
* progress without goals
* scale without complexity

In the progression window, you will even see all landmarks you have discovered, and you can even click it to learn more about the landmark. 

(insert the image nearbygame_progress.jpg)

This is where myself feeling exicted and having fun with. While I was working on this game, I learn so much about a state, its cities and landmarks. It actually feels very educational, in addition to feeling relax and calm. 

---

## Step 10: What I learned

This pivot taught me a few things:

### 1. Don’t force ideas

If something feels heavy, it usually is.

### 2. Remove dependencies

The more your product depends on other people, the harder it is to start.

### 3. Build for one user first

Then layer social on top.

### 4. AI is not just for coding

It’s a **thinking tool**:

* breaking down ideas
* challenging assumptions
* exploring alternatives

### 5. Speed matters

I went from:

* stuck idea → new concept → structured system → landing page

in a very short time.

---

## What’s next

Right now, NearbyGame is just a landing page. 

The next step is simple: Finish the first MVP and ship it. 

Then test:

> “Does this feel like something I want to come back to?”

I am actually very close to the MVP, stay tuned and come back later to learn more about the new NearbyGame. 

---

## Final thought

NearbyGame didn’t fail.

It evolved.

And the biggest shift wasn’t technical.

It was this:

> From organizing people…
> To creating a world where connection can happen naturally.

---

If you’re building something and feel stuck:

Try this:

Don’t ask “how do I build this?”
Ask:

> “What part of this idea is actually worth building?”

That’s where the real pivot begins.
